# Light — Class 8 Science (SAMPLE, synthetic curriculum outline)

## Learning outcomes
By the end of this chapter, students should be able to explain how light lets us see
objects, describe reflection from plane mirrors, state the laws of reflection, and
relate the structure of the human eye to how we perceive images.

## Reflection of light
Light travels in straight lines. When light falls on a smooth surface such as a plane
mirror, it bounces back — this is called reflection. The incoming ray is the incident
ray and the ray that bounces off is the reflected ray. The normal is an imaginary line
drawn perpendicular to the mirror at the point where the incident ray strikes.

## Laws of reflection
There are two laws of reflection. First, the angle of incidence is always equal to the
angle of reflection. Second, the incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal at the
point of incidence all lie in the same plane. These laws hold for every reflecting
surface, not just mirrors.

## Regular and diffused reflection
Reflection from a smooth, polished surface like a mirror is called regular reflection
and produces a clear image. Reflection from a rough surface such as a wall scatters
light in many directions — this is diffused reflection — which is why we do not see our
image in a wall even though light is reflecting off it.

## The human eye
The human eye works like a natural optical instrument. Light enters through the cornea,
passes through the pupil, and is focused by the lens onto the retina at the back of the
eye. The retina has light-sensitive cells that send signals to the brain through the
optic nerve. The iris controls the size of the pupil and therefore how much light
enters.

## Suggested classroom activities
Use a torch and a plane mirror to demonstrate the equal angles of incidence and
reflection on a protractor. Compare the clear image in a mirror with the lack of image
on a sheet of paper to distinguish regular from diffused reflection.
